## 1. Scope and organizing question

The manuscript develops a district-scale use case in which a distribution system operator (DSO) decides whether reinforcement of an MV/LV transformer or feeder group can be deferred, given uncertain electrification-driven load growth (EVs, heat pumps), distributed PV and batteries, and — critically — uncertain future availability of demand-side flexibility. The conventional probabilistic planning criterion P(E) ≤ P_crit is retained, but the single overload probability is replaced by alpha-indexed lower and upper bounds [P̲^α(E), P̄^α(E)] obtained by propagating a fuzzy controllability factor ρ̃_flex jointly with probabilistic (aleatory) inputs and an interval-valued model error through a black-box co-simulation chain.

This review therefore asks four questions of the literature. First, how has uncertainty been represented in distribution (and, secondarily, transmission) network expansion planning? Second, how has demand-side flexibility been treated as an alternative to reinforcement, and how has the deferral decision been evaluated under uncertainty? Third, what is known about the uncertainty of flexibility itself — the gap between technical and practically realized controllability? Fourth, which formalisms exist for combined aleatory–epistemic (imprecise) uncertainty, and how far have they penetrated power-system analysis and planning? The gap analysis in Section 10 sits at the intersection of the answers.

## 2. Distribution network expansion planning under uncertainty

Network expansion planning historically followed a deterministic, worst-case ("fit-and-forget") philosophy: forecast the most severe combination of maximum load and minimum generation and size the assets accordingly. Tavares & Soares (2020) document that several system operators still plan this way, and demonstrate on a real Portuguese MV network that the maximum-integration worst case does not even guarantee the most robust plan while inducing overinvestment. The recognition that expansion decisions face fundamental future uncertainty is old — Gorenstin et al. (1993) already framed power system expansion planning under uncertainty with scenario-based trade-off analysis — and the algorithmic tradition in distribution planning goes back at least to the multistage genetic-algorithm formulations of Miranda, Ranito & Proença (1994). Contemporary surveys (Sedghi et al., 2016; the 2024 *Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews* survey of uncertainty-management approaches for active distribution system planning) organize the modern field into four dominant uncertainty paradigms: probabilistic/stochastic programming over weighted scenarios; robust and adaptive-robust optimization over uncertainty sets (e.g., Amjady, Attarha, Dehghan & Conejo, 2018, for a distribution network with DERs); distributionally robust optimization over ambiguity sets; and information-gap decision theory (IGDT), applied to multiyear distribution reinforcement planning by Ahmadigorji, Amjady & Dehghan (2018) — hedging load, price, and cost uncertainty via a budget-of-uncertainty robustness radius — and to operational problems by Rabiee, Soroudi & Keane (2015). Risk-based formulations that pay explicit attention to uncertain demand and energy cost (Esmaeeli et al., 2017) and bi-level expansion models co-optimizing demand response (Asensio, Muñoz-Delgado & Contreras, 2017) extend the same optimization-centric tradition.

A separate, older strand introduced *fuzzy* representations directly into distribution expansion planning: Ramírez-Rosado & Domínguez-Navarro (2004) formulated a possibilistic multiobjective planning model optimizing fuzzy cost, fuzzy reliability, and exposure, determining the siting and sizing of future feeders and substations under possibility distributions rather than probabilities. This line established that expansion-relevant data can legitimately be modeled possibilistically when statistical evidence is absent, but it *replaces* the planning criterion with fuzzy objectives rather than bounding a probabilistic risk metric, and it predates the flexibility era.

The manuscript's decision metric belongs to a parallel, equally mainstream tradition: **risk-based planning criteria evaluated by probabilistic analysis**. Probabilistic load flow dates to Allan, Leite da Silva & Burchett (1981), with point-estimate accelerations by Su (2005); chance-constrained formulations bound violation probabilities directly, with Bienstock, Chertkov & Harnett (2014) the canonical risk-aware CC-OPF reference. Across all of these, however, the output is a *single* violation probability (or a feasibility verdict against a set), conditional on fully specified input distributions. None of the planning paradigms above produces *bounds* on the planning criterion indexed by the credibility of an epistemic assumption — which is precisely what a fuzzy-controllability p-box provides.

## 3. Flexibility as an alternative to reinforcement: non-wires alternatives and deferral

That demand-side resources can substitute for network capacity is now firmly established in economics, engineering, regulation, and practice. On the economics side, Poudineh & Jamasb (2014) framed distributed generation, storage, demand response, and efficiency as alternatives to grid capacity enhancement; Schachter & Mancarella (2016) critically reviewed real-options thinking for valuing investment flexibility in smart grids, and Schachter & Mancarella (2015) modeled demand-response contracts themselves as real options with explicit reliability risk. The closest economics-side neighbour to the manuscript is Schachter, Mancarella, Moriarty & Shaw (2016): a real-options framework and probabilistic tool for assessing DSR against conventional reinforcement in smart distribution networks, motivated exactly by the observation that heat pumps, EVs, and distributed generation make projections of required network capacity increasingly uncertain and that deterministic valuation risks stranded or insufficient assets. Full-text reading sharpens the contrast: the tool (implemented in spreadsheet form) simulates load-growth paths, values smart versus asset-based strategies as real options, and — notably — reports *physical* risk alongside cost, as single-valued yearly probabilities of excess load under each strategy; the paper closes by arguing that “probabilistic regulatory frameworks” are needed to give flexible investments their fair value. It thus reaches the manuscript's doorstep — deferral valued under uncertainty, with exceedance probabilities explicitly on the table — and stops exactly where the manuscript begins: every probability is a single value conditional on assumed distributions and point assumptions about DSR availability; there is no epistemic layer, no bounds, and no credibility indexing.

On the engineering-planning side, Spiliotis, Ramos Gutierrez & Belmans (2016) compared demand-flexibility contracts against physical expansion in distribution grids in a fully deterministic optimization (full-text check: uncertainty is not modeled at all; futures are explored only through a sensitivity sweep over the flexible-demand share); Klyapovskiy, You, Michiorri, Kariniotakis & Bindner (2019) proposed a techno-economic "value of flexibility" framework for comparing active elements (demand response, batteries, reconfiguration, dynamic line rating) against reinforcement on an actual 10 kV system, with uncertainty entering only through percentile-based worst-case demand and cost estimates; Mohtashami, Pudjianto & Strbac (2017) optimized multiyear distribution capacity with smart-grid technologies; and Asensio et al. (2017) embedded demand response in bi-level expansion planning. The single closest application neighbour to the manuscript — established by full-text analysis in this project — is **Tavares & Soares (2020)**: a distribution network reinforcement planning methodology that rigorously models per-resource flexibility (EV, storage, shiftable domestic loads) with intra- and inter-temporal constraints, uses a two-level (customer HEMS / DSO OPF-with-soft-limits) optimization to deploy flexibility against technical problems, and selects among scenario-specific reinforcement plans by a minimax rule on energy-not-supplied across three ENTSO-E TYNDP scenarios. Two of its explicit simplifications define the manuscript's opening: clients are assumed *fully available* to provide their flexibility to the DSO, and, because the scenarios assume flexibility will grow but do not specify how much, a flat 5% shiftable-load margin is adopted "given the lack of data." That is epistemic ignorance about practical controllability, acknowledged and then resolved by a point assumption.

On the deferral-timing frontier, Contreras-Ocaña, Siddiqi & Zhang (2018) co-optimize DER investment, DER operation, and the capacity-expansion year in a convex model (deferring a campus substation upgrade by roughly five years at the University of Washington), and explicitly list uncertainty in load growth and solar production as future work — i.e., even the most sophisticated NWA-timing formulation is deterministic.

Regulation and practice confirm the decision's real-world stakes. Article 32 of EU Directive 2019/944 requires member states to enable DSOs to procure flexibility services, explicitly including for deferral of network expansion; Anaya & Pollitt (2021) review international DSO flexibility-procurement projects; and Con Edison's Brooklyn–Queens Demand Management program (NY PSC Case 14-E-0302, approved December 2014) remains the emblematic non-wires deferral of a >$1bn substation upgrade via roughly 52 MW of procured demand-side relief — grey-literature sources, but authoritative for practice. Dutch practice-adjacent evidence sharpens both the stakes and the status quo: a Stedin-collaborated integrated heat–electricity study (Cicėnas, 2025) prices MV transformers at €25,000–35,000 per MVA and MV cables at €95,000–170,000 per km (figures confirmed by Stedin), and performs network reinforcement by a manual, iterative maximum-loading procedure — the deterministic practice baseline that probabilistic and bounded criteria improve upon. Across this entire strand, flexibility availability enters the deferral decision as a deterministic assumption, a scenario attribute, or a single probability; nowhere is the *credibility* of the availability assumption itself carried through to the decision metric.

## 4. The uncertainty of flexibility itself

Empirical work makes clear that realized flexibility diverges from technical flexibility for reasons that are largely human, contractual, and institutional: participation and opt-out, comfort tolerance, availability during the specific hours that matter, aggregator response reliability, rebound, and interoperability. Müller & Jansen (2019) demonstrated, over more than 300 inhabited residential buildings in the Danish EcoGrid 2.0 project, that heat-pump throttling can deliver load reductions of 40–65% of aggregate load (57 of 67 real experiments; full observed range 29–90%), predictable with a median absolute percentage error of 6.7% (quartiles 4.3%/12.3%) — while also finding that roughly 12% of heat pumps fail to respond to throttling commands (an empirical success rate of 0.88 must be applied), that the multi-device control chain “proved to be inconvenient and unreliable” even in a funded pilot, that rebound peaks reach roughly half the reduction magnitude unless actively damped (damping halves the peak but prolongs the rebound), and that deliverable throttling duration in cold weather is limited by household comfort settings. Notably, the pilot's market design mirrors the manuscript's use case exactly: a DSO forecasting imminent congestion opens a load-reduction market for the affected distribution grid zone. This single study therefore quantifies, for *enrolled* participants, the wedge between technical and practical controllability that the manuscript's fuzzy factor represents — while leaving the future enrollment and participation share, the truly deep uncertainty, unmeasured. EV-flexibility studies consistently identify participation rate, arrival/departure timing, and state of charge as dominant and only partially observable drivers of deliverable congestion relief. The most important methodological precedent is **Zeng, Wei, Zhao, Singh & Zhang (2018)**, who evaluate the capacity credit (CC) of demand response — via equivalent-firm-capacity-style adequacy metrics — with a *hybrid probabilistic–possibilistic* framework precisely because DR uncertainty arises from both exogenous (physical) and endogenous (human-related) factors, the latter not credibly reducible to probability distributions. Full-text reading confirms both the strength and the limits of the precedent: possibility distributions (membership functions) carry the endogenous customer-behavior uncertainty; the simulation even includes network remedial actions; and the authors themselves note the resulting CC “could be more readily used for the distribution system planning” — an aspiration the paper does not pursue. It is the strongest published argument that demand-side human factors are legitimately possibilistic, and it stops at a system-level adequacy metric: no feeder-level reinforcement-deferral decision, no probability bounds on a retained planning criterion, no co-simulation.

The pattern across Sections 3–4 is consistent: the field *documents* that future practical controllability is deeply uncertain, and *represents* it — when at all — probabilistically, by scenario, or by sensitivity sweep. Treating it as a first-class epistemic variable (a fuzzy number elicited from pilots, policy expectations, and stakeholder commitments) whose alpha-cuts index the planning risk metric has not been done.

## 5. Imprecise probability and hybrid aleatory–epistemic uncertainty: foundations

The manuscript's machinery has a mature ancestry in reliability engineering. The aleatory/epistemic distinction — irreducible variability versus reducible ignorance, and its status as a pragmatic modeling choice — is articulated canonically by Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen (2009); Ferson & Ginzburg (1996) argue foundationally that variability and ignorance require *different* propagation calculi (probability theory versus interval/bounds analysis), the cornerstone justification for any hybrid approach. Probability boxes — lower and upper bounds on a CDF, encoding aleatory spread and epistemic breadth simultaneously — were formalized, with construction methods and arithmetic, by Ferson, Kreinovich, Ginzburg, Myers & Sentz (2003); Beer, Ferson & Kreinovich (2013) survey imprecise probabilities in engineering, and Faes, Daub, Marelli, Patelli & Beer (2021) review the computational state of the art for p-box propagation. On the possibilistic side, fuzzy sets and possibility theory (Zadeh, 1965, 1978; Dubois & Prade) provide the membership-function representation of expert-based knowledge; Dubois, Foulloy, Mauris & Prade (2004) supply principled probability–possibility transformations useful for constructing membership functions from scattered estimates. The formal bridge that licenses the manuscript's construction — that the nested alpha-cuts of a fuzzy number are exactly a (generalized) p-box, unifying possibility distributions, random sets, and probability bounds — is Destercke, Dubois & Chojnacki (2008).

For propagation, the standard architecture is the **double loop**: an outer loop over the epistemic dimension (intervals, alpha-cuts, or Dempster–Shafer focal elements) and an inner Monte Carlo loop over aleatory variables, yielding bounds on the output probability measure. The fuzzy-alpha-cut-plus-Monte-Carlo "hybrid method" — directly analogous to the manuscript's architecture — is due to Baudrit, Guyonnet & Dubois (2006), with surrogate-accelerated variants for expensive models developed by Schöbi & Sudret (2017) in structural reliability. Baudrit et al. also demonstrate that naive post-processing of random-fuzzy output — collapsing the family of results into a single fuzzy interval — confounds variability with imprecision and can be overly conservative; this is a direct methodological argument for reporting the manuscript's result as alpha-indexed probability bounds rather than a single defuzzified number. Two computational facts from this literature matter for the manuscript: nested alpha-cuts permit sample reuse across levels, and when the output is *monotone* in the epistemic variable, interval propagation reduces exactly to evaluating the endpoints (the vertex method), collapsing the outer loop to two model runs per alpha level. Overload probability is plausibly monotone decreasing in the controllable share during demand-driven peaks (with caveats where rebound or reverse PV flow dominates), which — if verified — makes the double loop cheap even through an AC co-simulation.

## 6. Fuzzy, possibilistic, interval, evidence-theoretic, and p-box methods in power systems

Power systems has a long non-probabilistic tradition. Fuzzy load flow originates with the Porto school and is consolidated in Matos & Gouveia (2008), "The fuzzy power flow revisited," with a symmetric AC formulation in Gouveia & Matos (2009), fuzzy distribution power flow for weakly meshed systems in Bijwe & Viswanadha Raju (2006), and fuzzy DC load-flow arithmetic in Cortes-Carmona, Palma-Behnke & Jiménez-Estévez (2010). The standard taxonomies of uncertainty modeling in energy-system decisions are Soroudi & Amraee (2013) and the reviews of Aien, Rashidinejad & Fotuhi-Firuzabad (2014) — which also proposes an evidence-theory-based joint possibilistic–probabilistic power flow (whose belief/plausibility outputs are, formally, lower and upper probabilities on power-flow results — probability bounding in operational power flow avant la lettre) — and Aien, Hajebrahimi & Fotuhi-Firuzabad (2016). Hybrid possibilistic–probabilistic assessment in *distribution networks* is exemplified by Soroudi & Ehsan (2011), who model stochastic renewables probabilistically and controllable generation possibilistically to evaluate losses — methodologically the closest ancestor of the manuscript's aleatory/epistemic split — and by Soroudi (2012), a possibilistic–scenario model for DG impact assessment. Interval and hybrid stochastic–interval power flow (Guo, Gong, Bao & Wang, 2019) and hybrid possibilistic–probabilistic multi-energy flow (Dong, Sun, Huang, Li & Cheng, 2019) extend the family.

Probability boxes specifically entered power flow only very recently. Ding, Chen & Du (2021) introduce p-box-based uncertain power flow for wind-integrated systems (claiming the first introduction of p-boxes to power flow calculation); Amiri, Mohammadi, Karimi, Rastegar & Rostami (2021) develop probabilistic load flow on parameterized p-boxes explicitly for systems with insufficient information; and Li & Zhao (2022, *International Journal of Electrical Power & Energy Systems*) propose a p-box representation method for power flow under joint interval and probabilistic uncertainties (full-text check: modified IEEE 30-bus snapshot analysis). All three are *operational snapshot* analyses on test systems; none touches a planning criterion, a reinforcement decision, or demand-side flexibility. The nearest p-box use to expansion decisions is Miranda (2021, *IEEE Latin America Transactions*), which combines Monte Carlo with interval arithmetic over imprecise stochastic equipment data to evaluate reliability cost of expansion alternatives in composite (generation–transmission) systems — a partial neighbour in a regional venue, without flexibility, distribution scope, or co-simulation. Finally, on the distribution-network violation-risk side, Yao et al. (2022) propose an alpha-cut possibilistic evaluation of PV hosting capacity with an explicit violation-risk metric, showing that fuzzy reasoning on distribution violation risk is publishable and useful — but for hosting capacity, without a p-box output, a deferral decision, or co-simulation. The same group has since extended this line: Yao et al. (2026, *CSEE Journal of Power and Energy Systems*) model load demand possibilistically precisely because of its human-factor (endogenous) uncertainty, keep PV output as an interval, compute the possibility distribution of hosting capacity by alpha-cuts, and devote a section to how utilities should use a membership function rather than a crisp value in actual decision-making. This January-2026 publication is significant for positioning: it shows the possibilistic distribution-network line converging on exactly the decision-facing framing the manuscript adopts — while still lacking a p-box on a retained probabilistic criterion, any flexibility resource, a deferral decision, and co-simulation. Zeng et al. (2018), discussed in Section 4, completes this cluster on the demand-response side.

The conclusion of this section is double-edged and should be stated honestly in the manuscript: the *machinery* (hybrid fuzzy–Monte-Carlo propagation, p-boxes, alpha-cuts) is established, and its entry into power systems has begun; what does not exist is its use to *bound a retained probabilistic planning criterion* for a *flexibility-conditional reinforcement-deferral decision*.

## 7. Decision-making under deep uncertainty in grid and energy infrastructure

A distinct literature — decision-making under deep uncertainty (DMDU) — addresses exactly the epistemic situation the manuscript invokes: futures for which stakeholders cannot agree on a single probability law. Its canon includes robust decision making (Lempert, Popper & Bankes, 2003), dynamic adaptive policy pathways (Haasnoot, Kwakkel, Walker & ter Maat, 2013), info-gap decision theory (Ben-Haim, 2006), and the consolidated methodological volume of Marchau, Walker, Bloemen & Popper (2019). In power systems, deep uncertainty is operationalized in transmission expansion by Velloso, Pozo & Street's multi-scale distributionally robust formulation, which separates long-horizon expert scenarios from short-horizon ambiguity sets — a two-timescale logic that parallels the manuscript's aleatory/epistemic split — and in microgrid planning by Cao, Wang & Zeng's chance-constrained info-gap model — structurally the nearest published combination of a probabilistic criterion with severe non-probabilistic uncertainty: an IGDT outer layer maximizes robustness to long-term demand growth while chance constraints are enforced in operations (differentiated in detail in Section 10). Ahmadigorji et al. justify IGDT on the ground that neither accurate probability distributions nor membership functions can be obtained for rare, non-repeatable uncertainties — a position the manuscript refines rather than contradicts: probabilities where repeated data exist (aleatory inputs), membership functions where structured expert and pilot knowledge exists (controllability), bounds where the two meet. Tavares & Soares's (2020) minimax-ENS rule over three scenarios is itself a rudimentary DMDU device.

Relative to the manuscript, the DMDU family shares the diagnosis but not the prescription: RDM and pathways deliver robustness and regret verdicts across scenario ensembles; IGDT delivers a robustness radius around a nominal estimate. All of them *replace* the DSO's probabilistic acceptance criterion with a different decision language. The manuscript's distinguishing move — and its practical adoption argument — is that it *retains* the criterion P(E) ≤ P_crit in the exact form regulators and planning codes state it, and adds honest epistemic indexing on top. A method a DSO can use without renegotiating its planning norm is categorically easier to adopt than one requiring a new decision paradigm; this regulatory-compatibility argument should be made explicitly.

## 8. Co-simulation and uncertainty quantification of black-box model chains

The manuscript's computational vehicle — a modular chain of weather, adoption, technology, aggregation, and grid models exchanging only interface quantities — aligns with the smart-grid co-simulation paradigm: mosaik (Schütte, Scherfke & Tröschel; later consolidations by the OFFIS group) for modular component coupling, HELICS (Palmintier et al., 2017) for large transmission–distribution–communication federations, and the FMI standard for tool-agnostic model exchange. Uncertainty quantification within this paradigm has been recognized as necessary but remains, in published practice, external Monte-Carlo wrapping of deterministic federates (Steinbrink & Lehnhoff, 2016). Propagating *imprecise* (interval-, fuzzy-, or p-box-valued) uncertainty through a black-box co-simulation — with model-form error attached externally at an interface, preserving black-box compatibility, as the manuscript does with its grid-model output error interval — has no established power-systems precedent. This is a secondary but genuine element of novelty, and it connects the paper to the surrogate/propagation methods of Section 5 whose transfer from structural reliability to grid co-simulation is untested.

## 10. Critical gap analysis

**The gap, stated precisely.** No published work delivers alpha-indexed lower and upper bounds on a *retained* DSO planning criterion P(E) ≤ P_crit, where the epistemic driver is the future practical controllability of demand-side flexibility represented as a fuzzy number, the aleatory layer is propagated by Monte Carlo, model-form error is attached as an interval at a black-box interface, and the output supports a reinforcement-deferral decision (defer / defer-with-monitoring / reinforce / collect-data). Every edge of this intersection is populated — which is what makes the claim checkable — but the intersection is empty.

**How defensible is the gap? A critical self-assessment.** Four reviewer attacks are foreseeable, and the manuscript should be built to survive them rather than merely assert novelty.

*Attack 1: "The method is Baudrit's hybrid propagation applied to a load flow — incremental."* Largely true at the level of machinery: fuzzy-alpha-cut + Monte-Carlo propagation is twenty years old, and Zeng et al. (2018) already applied hybrid probabilistic–possibilistic modeling to demand response. The novelty therefore cannot be claimed in propagation. It must be claimed — and demonstrated — at the *decision layer*: the retained probabilistic criterion, the alpha-indexed acceptability logic, and the planner-facing quantities derived from it (Section 11). A methods-framed paper here is weak; a decision-framed paper is strong.

*Attack 2: "P-boxes in power systems already exist (2021–2022)."* Correct, and these papers should be cited prominently rather than discovered by a reviewer (so should Aien et al., 2014, whose evidence-theory power flow yielded belief/plausibility — lower/upper probability — outputs seven years earlier). The differentiation is scope and purpose: operational snapshot analysis on transmission test systems versus a planning-year risk criterion for a distribution investment decision. The manuscript is, on current evidence, the first *planning-decision* use of p-boxes in distribution systems and the first anywhere to make flexibility controllability the epistemic driver.

*Attack 3: "Epistemic distribution planning already exists (fuzzy 2004; IGDT)."* Correct, and the answer is the regulatory-compatibility argument of Section 7: those approaches replace the criterion; this approach keeps the criterion regulators actually state and bounds it. This is simultaneously the practical adoption case and the intellectual distinction, and it should be a named contribution, not a footnote.

*Attack 4: "The trapezoid (0.10, 0.25, 0.40, 0.60) is arbitrary."* The single most dangerous attack, because it targets the paper's hinge. An invented membership function invites the response that the resulting bounds are equally invented. Mitigation is available and cheap: derive the support and core from published realized-flexibility evidence (heat-pump pilot deliverability, EV smart-charging participation trials, BQDM realized-versus-procured relief) via a documented elicitation protocol, using established probability–possibility transformations (Dubois et al., 2004) where pilot statistics exist. The fuzzy number then becomes a reproducible synthesis of the evidence base rather than an assumption.

*Attack 5: “Cao et al. already combine a probabilistic criterion with severe non-probabilistic uncertainty.”* Structurally the nearest precedent, so pre-empt it explicitly. Cao, Wang & Zeng maximize an IGDT robustness radius on long-term demand growth subject to operational chance constraints being *satisfied* at fixed confidence. The manuscript inverts the construction: it does not enforce the criterion inside an optimization but *bounds the criterion itself*, reporting [P̲^α(E), P̄^α(E)] as a function of the credibility of the flexibility assumption, in an assessment supporting defer/monitor/reinforce/collect-data choices; the epistemic object is a fuzzy controllable share, not a demand-growth radius; and the setting is distribution reinforcement deferral through a black-box co-simulation, not microgrid DER sizing. Ferson & Tucker's (2008) reading of p-boxes as info-gap models makes the relationship precise: the alpha-indexed bounds generalize the robustness-radius answer while keeping the DSO's criterion in its native probabilistic form.

**A note on urgency.** Full-text verification of the supplied PDFs sharpened one point: the nearest neighbours are converging. The Yao group moved from a violation-risk metric (2022) to an explicit utility decision-making framing of possibilistic results (January 2026); p-box power flow went from zero to three papers in 2021–2022. The intersection the manuscript targets is still empty, but the surrounding cells are filling in fast — publishing sooner rather than later materially protects the novelty claim. The demand side of the gap is now equally documented: Varathan & Belwin Edward's (2024) first stated future-research direction is that most planning studies treat only DER-output and load variability, leaving other uncertainty sources under-researched, and Schachter et al. (2016) end by calling for probabilistic regulatory frameworks for flexible investments — both are citable mandates for exactly what the manuscript supplies.

**Residual risk.** Hybrid fuzzy–probabilistic planning work in second-tier and non-English venues (notably Chinese-language journals) is the most plausible location of an overlooked precedent. Before submission, run a final scan on: "fuzzy Monte Carlo distribution network planning," "possibilistic distribution expansion," "imprecise probability network planning," "credal network planning," "p-box reliability distribution network," and the Chinese-literature equivalents via Scopus/CNKI. Absence after that scan makes the novelty claim robust.

**Where the biggest gap lies — verdict after full-corpus reading.** Ranking the manuscript's candidate contributions by emptiness of the cell, pull from the field, and feasibility: (1) *Propagation machinery* is a commodity — hybrid fuzzy–Monte-Carlo propagation is two decades old (Baudrit et al., 2006), surrogate acceleration exists (Schöbi & Sudret, 2017; Faes et al., 2021), evidence-theory power flow produced lower/upper-probability outputs in 2014 (Aien et al.), and Zeng et al. (2018) already ran hybrid propagation on demand response; claim nothing here beyond competent implementation. (2) The *representation* — a fuzzy practical-controllability factor — is genuinely unoccupied (Zeng et al. fuzzify DR customer behavior for capacity credit; Yao et al. fuzzify load; Tavares & Soares assume full availability) and, with the pilot-anchored elicitation of §11.3, defensible; but alone it is an input-modeling choice, not a headline. (3) *Criterion bounding* — alpha-indexed [P̲, P̄] on the retained P(E) ≤ P_crit — is open: Cao et al. enforce chance constraints inside an IGDT wrapper but never bound the criterion; Miranda et al. bound a reliability *cost* and compare intervals at composite-transmission level; Schachter et al. compute single-valued exceedance probabilities and then call for probabilistic regulatory frameworks they do not supply. (4) **The decision layer is the emptiest cell with the strongest pull, and it is where the high-impact publication lies.** Across all 29 full texts and the wider reviewed literature, no work converts imprecise-probability output into planner-actionable quantities: the minimum practically-controllable flexibility share ρ* that keeps deferral compliant (a direct procurement and contracting target under Directive 2019/944 Art. 32, checkable against Müller-and-Jansen-style delivery statistics); the critical credibility level α* at which the upper bound crosses P_crit; the alpha-indexed deferral horizon (latest safe reinforcement year); and bound width read as monetizable value of information (fund pilots versus reinforce). Every strand's own authors point at exactly this hole — Tavares & Soares flag full availability as their simplification, Contreras-Ocaña et al. defer uncertainty to future work, Schachter et al. demand probabilistic regulatory evaluation, Varathan & Belwin Edward list under-researched uncertainty sources as direction one. The paper's winning configuration is therefore: decision layer as headline, criterion bounding as mechanism, fuzzy controllability as the honest input story, commodity propagation without method overclaim, and a decision-reversal case study (§11.2) as the demonstration.

## 11. Recommended sharpening of the project (for high impact)

The project as planned is publishable; the following adjustments are aimed at moving it from "solid application paper" to "high-impact decision-framework paper." They are deliberately modest in implementation cost.

**11.1 Reposition the contribution from propagation to decision.** Do not sell "we propagated hybrid uncertainty through a co-simulation" (Attack 1 wins). Sell "we give DSOs a defensible deferral criterion when flexibility availability is unknowable," operationalized through two new planner-facing quantities that fall out of the existing architecture at no extra computational cost: (a) the **critical alpha** α* — the credibility level at which P̄^α(E) crosses P_crit — interpretable as "how strongly one must believe in optimistic flexibility assumptions for deferral to remain acceptable"; and (b) the **minimum required controllability** ρ* — the smallest controllable share for which P(E) ≤ P_crit — whose membership value μ(ρ*) reads directly as the plausibility that deferral is safe, and which converts the analysis into a concrete procurement target ("the DSO must secure at least ρ* controllable flexibility, e.g., contractually, for deferral to be defensible"). These two numbers are quotable, novel, and immediately actionable; no existing method produces them.

**11.2 Add the decision-reversal benchmark — the money figure.** On the same case study, compare five treatments of ρ_flex: (i) deterministic full availability (the Tavares & Soares assumption); (ii) a single "best-estimate" probability distribution; (iii) a pure worst-case interval (robust); (iv) discrete scenario minimax; (v) the fuzzy/p-box approach. Construct the case so that the treatments *disagree about the decision*: e.g., the precise-probabilistic analysis certifies deferral while the alpha-indexed upper bound reveals the certification rests entirely on the optimistic tail of the flexibility assumption, and the worst-case interval forces reinforcement that the p-box shows unnecessary at reasonable credibility levels. A demonstrated decision reversal is what makes the epistemic honesty argument concrete instead of philosophical, and it is the figure editors and reviewers will remember.

**11.3 Ground the fuzzy number empirically.** Implement the mitigation of Attack 4 as a short, explicit elicitation subsection: sources (pilot studies, policy targets, stakeholder commitments), transformation method, resulting trapezoid, and a sensitivity check on its shape. This also strengthens the paper's reproducibility claim. A concrete, now source-verified protocol: decompose ρ̃_flex as (future enrollment share) × (delivery success given enrollment) × (deliverable fraction of enrolled flexible load during the critical window). Müller & Jansen (2019) pins the last two factors for heat pumps from 300+ real buildings — delivery success 0.88; deliverable core 0.40–0.65 of enrolled load with observed extremes 0.29–0.90; comfort-limited duration in cold weather — leaving only the enrollment share to expert judgment, policy expectation, and stakeholder commitments, which is exactly the component for which a fuzzy number is the honest representation. The example trapezoid (0.10, 0.25, 0.40, 0.60) can then be defended factor-by-factor rather than asserted, and each factor names the data (larger pilots, realized enrollment statistics) that would narrow it — feeding the value-of-information reading of recommendation 11.1.

**11.4 The recommended "slight change": add the deferral-horizon dimension.** The current plan evaluates one target year (2030 or 2035). Evaluate a small set of target years instead and report the **latest safe reinforcement year as a function of alpha** — an alpha-indexed deferral-horizon curve ("under core assumptions, reinforcement can wait until 2033; under the full plausible range, only until 2029"). This transforms the output from a binary verdict into a planning pathway, connects the paper to the adaptive-planning/DMDU literature (Section 7) that high-impact energy journals value, directly monetizes deferral (years deferred × annualized capex), and costs only additional co-simulation runs per year, not new methodology. It also naturally operationalizes the "defer with monitoring" decision row: monitoring triggers are the observable quantities that distinguish alpha levels over time.

**11.5 State and exploit the monotonicity shortcut; keep the model-uncertainty interval.** If P(E) is monotone in ρ_flex (argue it; verify numerically; caveat rebound- and reverse-flow-dominated regimes), each alpha-cut requires only endpoint propagation, and nested cuts permit sample reuse — making the double loop tractable through an AC co-simulation and preempting the "computationally infeasible" objection. This is worth stating as a minor methodological contribution. Retain the grid-model output-error interval: it makes the p-box genuinely two-sourced (input epistemic + model epistemic), differentiates the work from pure input-uncertainty studies, and demonstrates the black-box design claim.

**11.6 Venue.** The decision-framework framing fits *Applied Energy* (which published Zeng et al., Yao et al., Spiliotis et al., and Klyapovskiy et al. — the paper's natural conversation) as first choice; *IEEE Transactions on Power Systems* or *Sustainable Energy, Grids and Networks* / *IJEPES* if the framing tilts methodological; *Reliability Engineering & System Safety* only if reframed toward the reliability-engineering community (different related-work emphasis). Given recommendation 11.1, Applied Energy is the coherent target.
