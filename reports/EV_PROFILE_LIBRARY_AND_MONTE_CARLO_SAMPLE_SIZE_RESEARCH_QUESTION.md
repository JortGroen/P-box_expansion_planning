# Research Question: Joint Sample-Size Design for a Finite Profile Library and Monte Carlo Grid Simulation

**Status:** Research question; no sample-size values or statistical protocol are approved by this document.

We are developing a probabilistic distribution-grid reinforcement study using
annual 15-minute simulations. We need a statistically defensible method for
choosing two distinct sample sizes:

- `M`: the number of distinct annual EV/charge-point profiles generated by the
  ElaadNL stochastic profile generator and frozen in an empirical library.
- `N`: the number of complete whole-grid Monte Carlo year realizations used to
  estimate a downstream congestion measure.

An Elaad profile represents one complete annual trajectory for one EV or charge
point. Each whole-grid realization selects many such profiles according to the
modeled EV population, combines them with baseline demand, heat pumps, PV,
weather, adoption, and flexibility, and only then evaluates transformer
congestion. Direct empirical bootstrapping from the frozen Elaad library is the
primary method. Component-level percentiles and the Elaad dashboard's daily
p95 curve are not congestion measures.

The initial downstream target is provisionally the p95 quantile of an
integrated transformer outcome. We may later require p99, p99.9, a
fixed-threshold overload probability, or the probability of at least one
qualifying annual congestion episode. The method must therefore remain
applicable when the final congestion definition changes.

## Central Question

How should `M` and `N` be selected, monitored, and increased so that finite
profile-library uncertainty and Monte Carlo estimation uncertainty are
separately quantified and the downstream transformer result reaches a
predeclared precision or decision-stability criterion?

## Required Analysis

1. Clearly distinguish:
   - profile-library size `M`;
   - device/charge-point cohort size `K` within one grid realization;
   - whole-grid Monte Carlo sample size `N`;
   - finite-library uncertainty versus Monte Carlo sampling uncertainty.

2. Explain whether and under what special conditions `M = N` would be
   justified.

3. Derive or identify statistically justified methods for choosing `N` for:
   - p95;
   - p99;
   - p99.9;
   - probability of exceeding a fixed transformer threshold;
   - probability of at least one annual congestion episode.

4. For quantiles, consider:
   - asymptotic sample-quantile theory;
   - exact binomial/order-statistic confidence intervals;
   - bootstrap confidence intervals;
   - required pilot estimates such as the density near the target quantile;
   - failure modes for p99 and p99.9.

5. Use a 95% confidence level, `alpha = 0.05`. Do not confuse this with
   requiring a hypothesis-test p-value below 0.05. Explain what additional
   precision target, such as a confidence-interval half-width in transformer
   p.u., is needed to determine sample size.

6. Develop a method for selecting `M`. It must assess whether a finite empirical
   profile library adequately represents the effectively unlimited Elaad
   generator for the downstream transformer result. Consider:
   - nested library sizes;
   - disjoint held-out profile batches;
   - outer bootstrap or input-uncertainty methods;
   - sequential generation;
   - stopping criteria tied to transformer-result or decision stability;
   - whether standard CLT arguments can help size `M`.

7. Analyze direct-bootstrap replacement policies:
   - with versus without replacement inside one whole-grid realization;
   - repeated annual paths when `K > M`;
   - the effect on coincidence and aggregate tails;
   - how `M` should relate to `K`;
   - replacement across separate Monte Carlo realizations.

8. Determine when earlier Monte Carlo results can be reused:
   - increasing `N` while keeping the library and model fixed;
   - expanding `M` after simulations already exist;
   - moving from p95 to p99 or p99.9;
   - preserving common random numbers when reruns are required.

9. Propose a sequential algorithm with:
   - initial pilot `M_0, N_0`;
   - batch increments;
   - estimators and confidence intervals;
   - separate stopping criteria for `M` and `N`;
   - a maximum-compute escalation rule;
   - pseudocode suitable for implementation.

10. Discuss whether standard Monte Carlo remains appropriate for p99.9 or
    whether methods such as importance sampling, subset simulation,
    extreme-value modelling, or another rare-event method would be preferable.
    Any recommendation must respect complete annual time-series dependence and
    the nonlinear congestion-event detector.

11. Estimate laptop-scale storage and computational requirements for
    35,040-step annual profiles. Distinguish conceptual quantities such as
    `M * N` from the operations actually required by an efficient, chunked
    implementation.

## Evidence Requirements

Use authoritative statistical literature, preferably textbooks, peer-reviewed
papers, or official technical documentation. Provide exact citations, DOIs,
and URLs. Do not propose unexplained values such as "1,000 minimum," "2,000
target," or arbitrary percentage convergence tolerances. If numerical values
cannot be derived without pilot data, identify the exact pilot statistics and
decision tolerances required.

## Requested Deliverables

1. A recommended statistical design.
2. Separate formulas and stopping rules for `M` and `N`.
3. A worked illustrative example for p95, p99, and p99.9.
4. A table showing which samples can be reused as the analysis expands.
5. A laptop-feasibility assessment.
6. A manuscript-ready Methods paragraph.
7. A list of remaining PI decisions and the data required to make them.
